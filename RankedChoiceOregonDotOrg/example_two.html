How will votes be counted after the Ranked Choice Oregon ballot initiative is approved by Oregon voters?

To find out, let's imagine that a tiny town in Oregon is electing a new mayor ...

... from among these candidates ...

... whose names match those of the first four US presidents.

 

Let's imagine there are just twelve voters, so there are just twelve ranked ballots.

Each voter marks a ranked ballot ...

... to indicate their first choice ...

... second choice ...

... third choice ...

... and last choice.

Here are all twelve ballots being marked for their first choice ...

... second choice ...

... third choice ...

... and last choice.

Some simple rules interpret ballot marks ...

... that otherwise might be considered odd.

In fact the only way to spoil a ballot ...

... is to not be clear about whether an oval is marked or not marked.

Before we focus on each voter's first choice ...

... we look deep into the ballot preferences to discover candidates who are deeply unpopular.

For that we do what sports teams do. Each sports team competes against each other team one-on-one. That's one pair at a time.

In voting, instead of two teams competing, ...

... two candidates compete against each other, ...

... one pair of candidates at a time.

For these four candidates, ...

... there are six pairwise competitions.

...

In the pairwise contest between Washington and Adams ...

... a ballot that ranks Washington higher than Adams is counted as supporting Washington, ...

... and a ballot that ranks Adams higher than Washington is counted as supporting Adams.

When a voter does not indicate a preference between two candidates, the voter is saying "I'll let the other voters decide who wins this pairwise contest."

Here we see that more ballots support Adams than Washington.

So Adams wins this pairwise contest, and Washington loses this pairwise contest.

We keep track of these loses, including who the candidate lost to.

Notice that every mark on every ballot ...

... is being considered.

Sometimes there's a tie, which means that neither candidate lost.

[no change to this image]

Why are we doing these one-on-one, or pairwise, comparisons?

To create a safety net that prevents ...

... the kind of failure that happened in Burlington Vermont.


That failure to identify the most popular candidate ...

... caused the voters to repeal their use of ranked choice voting.


It happened because not all the marks ...

... on all the ballots were considered.

Now we start to eliminate candidates, one at a time. Is there a candidate ...

... who lost all three of the pairwise contests they were involve in? No, not in this elimination round.

So we focus on each ballot's highest-ranked candidate.





The candidate with the fewest of these votes ...

... is eliminated.

On the one ballot that ranks the eliminated candidate highest, ...





... we transfer the vote to the next-highest-ranked candidate.

Also we ignore the eliminated candidate in the scoreboard of pairwise losses.

Among the three remaining candidates, ...

... did any of them lose both of their one-on-one competitions? Yes. Madison lost to both Adams and Jefferson.

So we eliminate Madison because he is a pairwise losing candidate.

Now we have just two candidates. Unless there is an exact tie, ...

... one of them always has ...

... more transferred votes than the other.

Importantly, all the ballots that indicate a preference between the final two candidates are counted in this final round.

This final vote count always agrees with the pairwise loss information because it's the same comparison.

So we eliminate Adams ...

... and declare Jefferson as the winner, and the town's new mayor.

["Ranked Choice Including Pairwise Elimination (RCIPE)"]

This better counting method is called Ranked Choice Including Pairwise Elimination, or "recipe" for short.

["How do we count today?"]

Let's compare this with how we count ballots today.





Foolishly we assume the candidate with the most first-choice votes is most popular. In this case Adams would win.

What's the proof that Adams deserves to win instead of Jefferson?

We look at the one-on-one competition between them, without interference from the two less-popular candidates.

Jefferson would win over Adams, just like the earlier pairwise comparison revealed.

["Safety net needed?"]

Did we really need to do the pairwise comparisons?

["Fewest votes indicates least popular?"]

Yes, and to see why, let's suppose we eliminate the candidate with the fewest transfered votes in each round.





First, Washington would be eliminated because she has just one vote.

We transfer the ballot that initially supported Washington to its second choice, Jefferson.




Now Jefferson has the fewest transferred votes, so she is eliminated.

We transfer her supporting ballots to their next-highest choice.




Adams has more than half the transferred votes, ...

... so he wins.

["Fewest votes indicates least popular? Not always!"]

So yes, we need the safety net. It protects against the times when the candidate with the fewest transferred votes is not the least popular.

["1. Eliminate one candidate at a time. 2. If there is a pairwise losing candidate, eliminate them. 3. Otherwise, eliminate the candidate who is highest-ranked on the fewest ballots."]

Now you know how vote counting should be done.
