Simple Ranked Choice Voting

["Simple Ranked Choice Voting"]

Now let's look at simple ranked choice voting.

[similar to image earlier with stacks according to top choice, grey out choices except first choice]

As before, we start by looking at just the top choice on each ballot.

[stack ballots under their top-choice candidate, highlight candidate with fewest top-choice marks, grey out eliminated candidate]

Simple ranked choice voting assumes the candidate with the fewest votes is least popular, and deserves to be eliminated.

[focus on lack of any ballots ranking the eliminated candidate as their top choice]

In this case we eliminate the candidate who had no first-choice votes.

We count the ballots again, ...

... and we eliminate the candidate who now has the fewest votes.

[show full ballot that supported eliminated candidate]

This time there is one ballot that supported the eliminated candidate.

[show transfer of vote to second candidate on this one ballot]

The vote on this ballot gets transferred to the next-highest-ranked candidate.

[show transfer of vote to second candidate on this one ballot]

If that candidate was eliminated earlier, we transfer to the voter's next choice.

Again we count how many ballots support each of the remaining candidates.

[eliminated candidates still greyed out, highlight winning candidate]

This time, with just two candidates, the candidate who has more supporting ballots wins.

We have the winner based on repeatedly eliminating the candidate with the fewest transferred votes.

[words "Burlington failure" and map showing Burlington]

Alas, this simple version of ranked choice voting failed to identify the most popular candidate in the town of Burlington, Vermont in their 2009 election for mayor.

Improved Version of Ranked Choice Voting

[bring all ballots into view and unhighlight all marks]

To prevent such mistakes, let's look deeper into the ballots.

[show candidates competing one-on-one, maybe using soccer ball bouncing between them to indicate pairwise contests, start with 1 paired with 2 and 3 paired with 4, then rearrange to show 1 paired with 3 and 2 paired with 4, then rearrange to show 1 paired with 4 and 2 paired with 3]

The deeper preferences reveal who would win if the candidates competed one-on-one, the way soccer teams compete, which is one pair at a time.

[show one pair, candidates 1 and 2, and highlight ballots for those two candidates, then stack ballots according to which is preferred, and show winner above loser]

As we do these one-on-one, or pairwise, comparisons, ...

[To track wins, maybe use a symbol, such as a trophy, to represent each win. Here the counting is done in a list because that's what easier using HTML and CSS code.]

So in this round we eliminate the candidate ...

... with the fewest top-choice votes.

  • Washington: 0 wins

Again we look for ...

... a pairwise losing candidate.

  • Washington: 0 wins
  • Adams: 1 win
  • Jefferson: 0 wins

This time there is a pairwise losing candidate, ...

  • Washington: 0 wins
  • Adams: 1 win
  • Jefferson: 1 win

... someone with zero pairwise wins.

That means they lose ...

  • Washington: 0 wins
  • Adams: 2 wins
  • Jefferson: 1 win

... all their pairwise contests.

So we eliminate this pairwise losing candidate.

This leaves us with just two remaining candidates.

In this situation we have two ballots ...

... on which we transfer the vote to the voter's next-highest remaining choice.

Between the two remaining candidates, the candidate with more votes wins.

This time we have the correct winner.

[question mark]

How do we know?

Because if the two kinds of winners competed without interference from non-winning candidates, the correct winner would win with more votes.
