Makerbiz Tax Credit Will Strengthen Portland's Economy

It's obvious that Portland-area “maker” businesses such as Columbia Sportswear, Ampere Computing, Laika, and OMSI bring lots of money into the Portland economy.  So it's easy for them to arrange special tax breaks.

What gets overlooked is that small “maker” businesses such as small Portland manufacturers, software-based businesses, single-location restaurants (Le Pigeon, Portland City Grill, and hundreds more), local publishers, local self-employed artists and performers, and local performance venues also bring significant amounts of money into Portland from outside the city.

Portland has thousands of small maker businesses.  To make it easier for them to grow, and to get more customers, and to hire more employees, they should be getting a special “makerbiz” tax credit.  Instead of arranging the makerbiz tax credit one business at a time, a small maker business will simply claim the tax credit on their business tax form.  Qualification will be based on their NAISC code and, if needed, by looking at their public domain-specific website to verify they make and sell something that has value to customers outside the Pacific Northwest.

The makerbiz tax credit will slow down, and even reverse, the rate at which Portland maker businesses have been moving out of Portland to avoid Portland's high business tax rates, and to avoid Oregon's high business tax burden.  Plus it will help some women and minority entrepreneurs to grow their business without the cost of administering yet another taxpayer-funded “assistance program.”

Maker businesses

“Maker” businesses make things that previously did not already exist.

Large maker businesses, and some high-profile maker businesses, already get tax breaks.

Currently single-location maker businesses struggle to stay in business.

Represented by task force members, and already get tax breaks: Ampere Computing, Columbia Sportswear, Tillamook Creamery, Laika, Oregon Museum of Science and Industry, Portland Art Museum, Portland Trail Blazers

Represented by task force members, and qualify for the “makerbiz” tax credit:  Mother's Bistro, ZGF Architects

Not represented by task force members, but would qualify for “makerbiz” tax credit:  Le Pigeon, Seasons & Regions, Andina, Portland City Grill, (and many other single-location restaurants listed at NewsHereNow.com), Annie Bloom's Books (and other independent bookstores listed at NewsHereNow.com), many software-based businesses, small Portland manufacturers, local publishers, local performance venues

Other businesses

Businesses that are between the “maker” and “taker” extremes mostly sell products and services to local customers, similar products and services are sold by businesses in other states, and typically they do not make anything that didn't already exist.

This category includes: retail, transportation, communication,

They do not bring much money into Oregon from outside of Oregon.

Represented by task force members:  Port of Portland, Legacy Health, Society Hotel in Old Town, City of Roses Disposal & Recycling

Taker businesses

Taker businesses mostly sell to Oregon customers, do not create things that would be of value outside Oregon, and typically sell things that also are available in nearly every other state within the United States.

Represented by task force members:  The Standard, Wells Fargo Bank, US Bank, Melvin Mark Brokerage, Moda Health Insurance, Fortuna Group, Schwabe, Hacienda CDC, TMT Development, Willamette Management

Government entities and non-profit organizations

Businesses pay business taxes (including payroll taxes) that fund government activities such as hiring police officers, funding courtrooms, funding social services to homeless and drug-addicted people.

Represented by task force members:  City of Portland, Metro Council, Travel Portland, Portland State University, Portland Metro Chamber, Business for a Better Portland, Micro Enterprise Services of Oregon, Verde, Native American Youth and Family Services, Prosper Portland, Asian Pacific American Network of Oregon, Black Business Association of Oregon, Hispanic Metro Chamber, Philippine American Chamber of Commerce of Oregon and Greater Portland Economic Development District, Central City Concern, The Urban League

How important are maker businesses?  If Portland, and Oregon, had no maker businesses, including Intel and Nike, all of Portland's real estate businesses, law firms, banks, and insurance companies would have no customers.  And most mortgage-paying homeowners would become homeless.  Burglary and robbery would become the most common profession.  And police departments would be unable to protect independently wealthy residents and employees who remotely work for out-of-state businesses.  In other words, the money earned by maker businesses is what powers our Oregon economy.  Let's give all of them, not just the big ones, a tax break.  This simple tax break will help them grow, and bring yet more money into the Oregon economy.
