Request to Add New RCTab Overvote Rule Named “Temporarily Inactive When Multiple Continuing”

This document describes a new Overvote Rule that should be added to the RCTab software maintained by the Ranked Choice Voting Resource Center (RCVRC).

This new rule temporarily counts a ballot as inactive if, during the current counting round, two or more continuing (not yet eliminated) candidates are marked at the same rank column.  The ballot is restored to active when just one of the overvoted candidates is continuing.  Only after all the overvoted candidates in a column are eliminated does counting progress to the next rank column.

Why Add This New Overvote Rule?

Here are reasons why this new overvote rule needs to be added.

  • Counts overvotes in a way that better honors the voter's clearly indicated intent.  In contrast, all three existing overvote rules — “Exhaust immediately” and “Exhaust if multiple continuing” and “Always skip to next mark” — violate the voter's intent.
  • Allows a voter to mark any oval for each candidate without causing any skipping or exhausting during the counting.
  • Portland (Oregon) would have chosen this better overvote rule when we adopted ranked choice voting for our mayoral and city-council elections, and we are likely to switch to this better overvote rule after it becomes available.  (This adoption is likely to be delayed by the cost of re-educating voters, and the cost of upgrading the contract with the election-system vendor.)
  • Other cities and states that adopt ranked choice voting will save money on voter education because teaching voters to avoid overvotes will become much less important!
  • Allows a voter to rank their most-disliked candidate below all other candidates, even when there are more candidates than rank columns.  This advantage would have been especially useful in the 2024 Portland (Oregon) mayoral election because the two biggest-money-backed candidates were strongly disliked.  Many voters would have liked to rank their most-disliked candidate at rank 6, rank their next-most-disliked candidate at rank 5, and rank numerous other candidates in ranks 1 through 4.  Yet if any voter did this, their ballot could have counted as support for one of their two most-disliked candidates in opposition to a higher-ranked candidate.
  • A ballot is never counted for a voter's clearly disliked candidate instead of another continuing candidate who the voter has clearly ranked higher.
  • Oregon's 2024 ballot measure 117, which would have adopted ranked choice voting for some Oregon elections (if it had passed), omitted any mention of “overvotes” (or how to count them) so a better overvote rule (such as this one) could be adopted when it became available (without needing to revise the counting rules).  This high-profile legal wording reveals that lots of election experts in Oregon want this better overvote rule.
  • The existing option named “Always skip to next mark” too easily counts the ballot for candidates who are ranked lower than the overvoted candidates, even while one or more of the higher-ranked overvoted candidates are still continuing.
  • The existing options “Exhaust immediately” and “Exhaust if multiple continuing” dismiss all the remaining voter's marks instead of waiting for eliminations that might cause an overvoted column to become a mark for just one candidate.
  • Overcomes one of the two biggest criticisms of ranked choice voting, which is that ranked-choice ballots are difficult to learn how to mark.

    • If anyone still thinks existing overvote rules are adequate, please look at the webpage here — https://votefair.org/ballot_marking_advice.html — which I created to explain to Portland voters how to mark ballots in the 2024 Portland mayoral and city-council elections (which both used ranked choice voting).  It clarifies why simple advice such as “don't mark the ones you don't like” isn't helpful when there are lots of candidates, including some who are strongly disliked.

    Simple descriptive example

    Here's a simple scenario that explains how this overvote rule works.

    Clarification:  This description, and this entire document, refers to a voter marking ovals in rank columns.  This convention applies to elections in the United States.  In Australia a voter writes a number in the empty box next to each candidate's name, so in that case an overvote occurs when the voter uses the same number more than once.

    • Assume a ballot ranks candidates C and D in the same rank column.
    • Assume the candidates ranked higher than candidates C and D have been eliminated.
    • For each counting round in which candidate C is continuing (not yet eliminated), and candidate D has been eliminated, count the ballot for candidate C.
    • For each counting round in which candidate C has been eliminated, and candidate D is continuing, count the ballot for candidate D.
    • For each counting round in which both C and D are continuing, count this ballot as temporarily inactive, in the category Inactive Ballot by Overvote.
    • For each counting round in which both candidates C and D have been eliminated, use the ranking information for the candidates who are ranked lower than candidates C and D.

      Summary

      Here are reminders why adding this overvote rule, Temporarily Inactive When Multiple Continuing, to the RCTab software is so important.

      • It will save lots of money otherwise wasted on voter education trying to teach voters to resist the urge to simply and clearly indicate their actual preferences.
      • The lack of a primary election in Portland revealed increased frustration for marking a ranked choice ballot when it has only six rank columns, involves more than 10 candidates, has one or two front-runner candidates who are strongly disliked, and uses an existing overvote rule that easily betrays the voter's clear intent.
      • Most importantly, this simple overvote rule will reduce voter confusion and speed up adoption of ranked choice voting!
